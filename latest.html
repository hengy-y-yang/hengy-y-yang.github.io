Latest News
Bingleys moving to Longbourn

AUTUMN 1

WORD GOT AROUND today that a new family just came to settle at Longbourn. They include Mr. Bingley, his sister Ms. Bingley, and his good friend Mr. Darcy.

Netherfield, a place deserted for ages, was looked over by Mr. Bingley, and he allegedly said it was a very fine house. He will surely settle into the estate before September 29th.

It has been rumored that Mr. Bingley earns upwards of 5 thousand pounds per year.

Dining with the Bingleys

AUTUMN 2

JUST TODAY, THE Bennets dined with Mr. Bingley and his companions at Netherfield Park. There were only five attendees on Mr. Bingley's side. Jane and Mr. Bingley started talking very soon after the Bennets arrived, and they very quickly fall in love.

One of our viewers say that Mr. Bingley was "good looking and gentlemanlike" (Austen 6). They also report that Mr. Darcy, a close friend of Mr. Bingley, was found out to "be proud, to be above his company, and above being pleased" (Austen 6).

Although everyone avoided Mr. Darcy after discovering his character, before the fact they admired him very much.

How Bad is Darcy's Pride?

AUTUMN 3

MR. DARCY'S PRIDE has revealed itself to be overwhelmingly strong. An inside person reports that Mrs. Bennet loved to "relate... the shocking rudeness of Mr. Darcy" (Austen 8). She said that "'he is a most disagreeable, horrid man[; s]o high and so conceited that there was no enduring him!'" (Austen 8).

An attendee of the dinner reported that between "[Bingley] and Darcy there was a very steady friendship, in spite of a great opposition of character" (Austen 10).

Why is Darcy such a good friend of Bingley? Their personalities are worlds apart, so they must have some other connection. Check back for updates!

Visiting at Longbourn Reveals Love?

AUTUMN 4

THE NETHERFIELD PARTY dined with Longbourn today. Most people had fun, although some of Mr. Bingley's relations ridicule the Bennet family.

Important news was just revealed by an insider, who says that Mr. Darcy has a fancy for Elizabeth! He was reported to have asked Eliza to dance during the party, but was soundly rejected on account of his prideful manners (Austen 17). Mr. Darcy, who stepped back but was not offended, said to Ms. Bingley that he "has been meditating on the very greate please which a pair of fine eyes in the face of a pretty woman can bestow" (Austen 17).

Later, Mr. Darcy reveals that he does admire Elizabeth. Could this be the blossoming of love? Tune in to find out.

The Stay at Netherfield

AUTUMN 5

YESTERDAY, JANE ARRIVED sick to Netherfield due to her travel in the rain. It is rumored that Mrs. Bennet sent her for a meeting at Netherfield on horseback instead of carriage so that she could not come back easily.

Elizabeth hurried to the Netherfield estate. She allegedly spent most of her time there with Jane, making sure she was well. An insider reported that Mr. Bingley's relatives ridiculed her greatly in her absence, but Darcy still admires her, saying her eyes were "'brightened by the exercise'" (Austen 24).

Mrs. Bennet went to Netherfield estate to check on her daughter this morning. Apparently she wants Jane to keep being sick so Jane can stay at Netherfield and create connections with its members.

The Netherfield Stay: Part 2

WINTER 1

DURING MRS. BENNET'S visit, an insider reports that she had no shortage of meanness to Mr. Darcy. While talking on the subject of "good breeding", Mrs. Bennet allegedly said that "'those persons who fancy themselves very important and never open their mouths, quite mistake the matter'" (Austen 29).

This is obviously a jab at Mr. Darcy, who Mrs. Bennet is trying to frame as someone too prideful for their own good. She also tries to frame Jane in a more favorable light, to encourage Mr. Bingley's feelings of regard toward her.

It has been rumored that Ms. Bingley also has feelings for Mr. Darcy. She tries to get Darcy's attention through many schemes, such as berating him with questions (Austen 31-32). She also tries to make his love seem imprudent by ridiculing Elizabeth's relations, to mostly no avail (Austen 35).

Mr. Collins Proposes to Elizabeth

WINTER 3

AN INSIDER HAS just informed that Mr. Collins, a clergyman many consider to be socially insensitive, has proposed to Elizabeth.

Elizabeth soundly rejected his proposal, but Mr. Collins, no doubt too eccentric to notice her refusal, keeps encouraging.

Elizabeth, frustrated at him, says "[i]f what I have hitherto said can appear to you in the form of encouragement, I know not how to express my refusal" (Austen 75). Mr. Collins, eventually convinced, decides to marry Charlotte. It is rumored that Charloote and Elizabeth are not good friends anymore.

The Late Mr. Darcy's Will was disregarded?

WINTER 4

Word just came in today that Mr. Darcy, in all of his selfishness, actually took away money from Mr. Wickham by disobeying his father's will!

Mr. Wickham's father was a steward for the late Mr. Darcy. Mr. Darcy was very fond of Wickham, and devoted a large part of his will to him. However, Mr. Wickham exclaims that after the late Mr. Darcy's death Mr. Darcy actually stopped him from receiving the benefits.

How someone could be so evil, who knows? This definitely explains Wickham's and Darcy's dislike of each other, and their stiff greeting and perceived hostility in Meryton.

The Bingleys Leave the Neighbourhood of Longbourn

WINTER 5

Word just came and Bingley were getting very close, urgent business pulls Bingley to London. The Bennets wish for Mr. Bingley's immediate return, but an insider says their hopes dropped when Ms. Bingley writes to them, saying that they may not come back during winter (91).

Does Mr. Bingley actually love Jane? Is he indifferent, or is this a plot by the despicable Mr. Darcy to keep them apart?

Elizabeth Travels to Rosings, Gets Proposal from Mr. Darcy?!?

ELIZABETH, AS A sort of visit, travels to Rosings and lives with Mr. and Mrs. Collins. Darcy and his much mor agreeable cousin Colonel Fitzwilliam, meanwhile, are staying at Rosings.

It is rumored that Mr. Darcy keeps trying to "accidentally" meet Elizabeth because of a hidden, albeit intense, regard.

THIS JUST IN: Darcy has proposed to Elizabeth! Of couse the rational Elizabeth rejected him, just as she would reject anyone brimming with pride. It seems that maybe Mr. Darcy proposed partly to insult Elizabeth's family's impropriety and middling social class.

Lydia Will Go To Brighton to Accompany the Regiment

SPRING 3

JUST TODAY, AN insider has informed us that Lydia will go to Brighton with the officers. Is this because she has regard for one of them?

After the regiment leaves, Elizabeth can only rejoice on Wickham leaving and her trip to the Lakes.

UPDATE: Word has spread that Elizabeth and the Gardiners will go to Derbyshire, near the Pemberley Estate, instead.

Elizabeth almost Falls in Love with Darcy at Pemberley Estate?

SUMMER 1

ELIZABETH HAS SET out to visit Derbyshire, as was previously planned. However, on arriving there, her uncle and aunt want to visit Pemberley Estate. Elizabeth is still embarrassed at how she mistreated the benevolent Darcy, but the locals assure her that he was somewhere else.

On visiting the mansion, Elizabeth wonders at the relatively modest but elegant furnishing, and surmises that if not for her prejudice, she would be living here too. The servants' steadfast praise of Mr. Darcy also raises her regard for him, as she realizes that he is a kind and generous person, just unaccustomed to suppressing pride. Right before leaving Pemberley Estate, Elizabeth bumps into Darcy, who arrived before his friends. They talk awkwardly with each other, embarrassed at their previous dispositions. Eventually, they get to chatting, and it is arranged that Elizabeth will meet Georgiana Darcy too.

During her meetings with Georgiana, Elizabeth finds out that her air of dignity actually comes from her shyness rather than pride. Together, they talk and become friends.

Lydia Eloped with Wickham?!?

SUMMER 2

LYDIA HAS JUST left the regiment in Brighton with Wickham, reporters inform. She was supposed to stay with Mr. and Mrs. Forester, but only left a note saying that they left.

Elizabeth hurried back from Derbyshire to help with the plan to locate them, but the track was lost in London. This will be a big blow to the Bennets' reputation if Lydia is not found.

THIS JUST IN: An insider reports that Lydia and Wickham were encouraged to marry as fast as possible. They say that Darcy was involved greatly in locating them and persuading them to take the route with least damage to the family.

Darcy's Involvement in Finding Lydia

AUTUMN 6

SINCE LAST ARTICLE, Another insider has reported on the elopement of Lydia and Wickham. According to them, Darcy played a big role in facilitating the marriage. First, he located and bribed Mrs. Younge, who knew where they were staying.

Then, he actually covered all the debt of Mr. Wickham, and provided the money for the marriage settlement. However, Mr. Gardiner was credited with helping.

In the end, Darcy's role was mostly clandestine in helping the marriage. Only Elizabeth, the Gardiners, and Lydia should know about it.

Jane Engaged with Mr. Bingley? Impossible!

AUTUMN 7

JANE HAS FINALLY admitted that she still loves Bingley from the bottom of her heart. It is said that when all others were doing their own thing, Jane and Bingley conversed in the living room.

As they chatted, they realized that each person had unabated love for the other, and as they sorted out their misunderstandings, they agreed on an engagement.

This must bring a lot of joy to both the Bennets and Mr. Darcy, whose benevolence we have seen with our own eyes.

Elizabeth's Regard for Mr. Darcy

AUTUMN 8

YESTERDAY, DURING A PRIVATE walk around the neighbourhood of Longbourn, Elizabeth confesses her love for Darcy through a compliment to his kindness. He is so happy that he tells her he wanted to confess to her again for ages, but would not stand the embarrassment.

In interviews with Propriety, Elizabeth says Darcy's real character changed her point of view. Darcy said that Elizabeth's lively mind, or what Elizabeth calls impertinence, caught his eye the most. He enjoyed the blunt way she talks to him, doing away civility for straightforwardness.

Mr. Bennet, when interviewed, said that he doubted whether Elizabeth could be happy in such a marriage, but in the end noticed that her feelings for Darcy have changed very much since they went to Rosings. He realized that her love stemmed from an admiration of his character, and so did Darcy's. Mrs. Bennet declined to comment, citing her happiness as the main reason. Thanks for sticking with us!
